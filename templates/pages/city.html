How these numbers are calculated

Where the boundaries and taxes come from

Parcel shapes are Skagit County’s own GIS parcel layer. Acreage, current use, and taxes are from the Skagit County Assessor for each active parcel inside {{ city.name }} city limits. Zoning districts come from the Washington Zoning Atlas spatial database, joined to each parcel by parcel ID and area overlap.

Why zoning determines which scenarios appear

Each parcel only shows development scenarios that its zoning district actually permits under current local zoning. An R-1 parcel won’t show “Shops + Apartments” because that’s not legal there. A CBD parcel won’t show “Add a Home” for the same reason. The scenarios reflect real legal ceilings, not just physical ones.

How a scenario’s tax/acre is set

For each scenario, we take the zone-aware median tax per acre already produced by similar parcels in {{ city.name }} today. If a zone has too few examples, the model falls back to compatible local zones, then citywide examples.

The gain formula

annual gain = (scenario tax/acre − current tax/acre) × acres, then 10-year gain = annual gain × 10 × 50% buildout factor. The 50% factor assumes only half of a parcel’s potential is realized within 10 years.

Total vs. City-only revenue

Total public revenue is the full property tax bill (city, county, schools, fire, hospital, etc.). City-only revenue multiplies that by each parcel’s actual share that flows to {{ city.name }}, computed from its specific tax levy code.

The citywide number

The headline figure sums the 10-year gain for every visible parcel where at least one scenario would outperform its current tax/acre — it updates as you change the revenue view or the land-use filter.
